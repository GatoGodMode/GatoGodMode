## Disruption Model: From Scattered Manual Parts to One Capture-Native Workstation

Collectors preparing cards and coins for submission — or evaluating raw inventory — typically assemble capability from unrelated SKUs: centering templates, desk magnifiers, cleaning kits, holders, and mailers. Each piece works in isolation; **none was designed to feed modern AI, pricing APIs, or image search** with consistent geometry, lighting, and motion.

No off-the-shelf "module" today closes that gap. The closest options are analog tools plus whatever phone or browser workflow the user improvises. That caps signal quality for both human judgment and machine perception.

Moun is positioned as **the physical layer that makes existing digital tools measurably more effective**: stable pose, repeatable illumination, and video-friendly motion so AI grading flows, price-check tools, and image search see the same asset the way a pro bench would.

### Three truths about today's fragmented retail

1. **Manual by default** — magnifiers, grids, and gauges depend on operator skill, fatigue, and ambient light, not a calibrated envelope
2. **Mixed modules** — cleaning, inspection, protection, and shipping are separate purchases with no shared reference frame
3. **No intelligence bridge** — retail SKUs do not connect to pricing graphs, reverse image search, or video-native AI; the user bridges the gap by hand

### What the incumbents actually are

Ecommerce is full of folding photo studios, fabric light tents, and C-clamp overhead arms with bolt-on ring LEDs. They fill the "better than a desk lamp" gap for small-product photography — but they are a weak structural match for open, trust-heavy card trading or repeatable slab-grade capture. Tents wall off the specimen; overhead crane rigs swing into the phone screen. Moun's open staging, clampless placement, and reflective-media lighting intent put it in a different category.

## Fragmented vs. Integrated

| Axis | Fragmented retail stack | Integrated station |
|---|---|---|
| **Physical setup** | Separate magnifier, template, lamp, and phone rig; constant re-alignment | One envelope: staged specimen, coordinated lighting, predictable camera path |
| **Data readiness** | Images and video vary by user; AI and search see inconsistent inputs | Repeatable capture profile designed to interoperate with external tools |
| **Operator load** | High touch — every tool swap is another chance to bump, smudge, or mis-frame | Lower context switching; workflow stays on the specimen and the screen |
| **Category gap** | No SKU combines bench physics with export-quality digital capture intent | Explicit positioning as the missing module between raw goods and the AI/market stack |

## Where Integration Pays: Four Opportunity Pillars

1. **AI at full strength** — models (including video-first grading flows) need stable framing, controlled glare, and repeatable tilt; a purpose-built station supplies what a handheld phone on a desk cannot
2. **Marketplace comps** — price checkers and market-depth tools work best when SKU identity and condition context can be trusted; consistent capture reduces mis-clicks and ambiguous listings
3. **Reverse image search** — sharp corners, true color, and square-on geometry maximize match quality versus ad-hoc snapshots
4. **Video-native grading** — when workflows ask for multi-angle passes, one coordinated motion-and-lighting rig beats restaging the specimen for every clip

## Demand Signals

Across a five-year window, U.S. web search interest for "Pokémon" ([Google Trends](https://trends.google.com/trends/explore?geo=US&q=pokemon)) retreated from the late-2022/early-2023 spike but remains structurally elevated versus the early-2021 baseline, with a renewed climb into 2025–2026 approaching prior highs. YouTube-channel trends pulse in parallel — video discovery rides the same keyword.

### Grading volume is measured in millions per month

- ~**2.97 million cards** graded across major grading companies — up ~19% month-over-month, up nearly 50% year-over-year

## The Economics Gap: DIY Prep Spend vs. Grading Fees

Collectors comparison-shop desk magnifiers (~$35–120), centering aids (~$10–25), cleaning kits and holders (~$25–90), and add-on ring lights or tripods (~$25–65) — a serious piecemeal hobbyist stack often lands **~$95–300 combined** — while simultaneously routing large sums through PSA, CGC, Beckett, TAG, and peers.

PSA's official February 2026 bulletin listed card submission prices from **$24.99 (Value Bulk) to $79.99 (Regular)** per card ([psacard.com](https://www.psacard.com/info/submission-updates)). At ~3M items/month industry-wide, even conservative hypothetical average fees imply **order-of-magnitude $75M–$180M in monthly service spend** (sensitivity illustration only — not reported revenue):

| Hypothetical average fee | Implied monthly spend (~2.97M items) |
|---|---|
| $25 | ~$74M |
| $40 | ~$119M |
| $60 | ~$178M |

**Representative DIY prep basket (directional retail spot-checks):**

| Item | Typical range |
|---|---|
| LED magnifier / task inspection lamp | $35–120 |
| Centering / comparison aid | $10–25 |
| Cleaning kit + sleeves / semi-rigid holders | $25–90 |

**The takeaway:** hardware that improves pre-submission capture is a rounding error next to grading invoices — but it materially affects match quality, resubmission rates, and time-to-list. Moun monetizes once on the prep-and-capture layer; grading incumbents monetize per item at scale. Small improvements in imagery and operator consistency compound across the entire downstream stack.

## Quick Comps Beat Manual Typing

Same-day secondary-market listings for identical items coexist at wildly different price points — desk shots, handheld snaps, and stand displays all competing for buyer trust. Auctions can diverge sharply from buy-it-now medians in the same week.

Shops and collectors need that check whether the item is raw or already encapsulated: re-listing, verification, insurance photos, and show-floor trades all reward the same thing — **fast, trustworthy identification and price context**.

Typing full Pokémon names, set names, card numbers, and variant filters is slow and error-prone under con-floor or counter pressure. Image-recognition flows on major platforms collapse that path **when the camera feed is legible** — routing to structured product pages and live comps in seconds.

The handset is already the sensor. **Moun supplies the environment** — even light, top-down geometry, controlled glare — so automated matchers and human review agree on the same asset, raw or slabbed.
